Oak Bluffs Historic Higlands Map

The Oak Bluffs Historic Highlands Archaeology project introduces the Historic Highlands Property Map; a web-based, interactive map that uses publicly available deed documents to represent the history of landownership in the Highlands area of Oak Bluffs, MA, on the island of Martha’s Vineyard. Oak Bluffs has historically been a pluralistic, multi-ethnic and multi-racial, summer and year-round community, famously noted for being a popular place for African American vacationers1.

This map spatializes, or represents visually in geographic space, 19th and 20th century property records held in the Dukes County Registry of Deeds (Link). Spatializing these records allows visitors to view aspects of the Highlands community at different points in time, including the size of the community, distribution of owned lots, sale / purchase prices, and demographic information on residents. Many deed records on this map have been combined with census records, on individuals and families and the community itself. This additional information can be explored through links to chain of title records in each of the popups that appear for the properties.

Visitors to the Historic Highlands Archaeology Project website can log into the database with the username guest and password oakbluffs or make their own account. We will continually be adding information to these records and invite any visitors to share their stories, knowledge, and understandings with our team.

After opening the interactive Historic Highlands Property Map, visitors will see most of the lots owned and lived on in the Highlands from 1870, when lots were first sold in the area, to 1959, when our project ends. To see all the properties, move the slider on the right side of the map to the end. This “time slider” tool allows visitors to explore the Vineyard Highlands community at different points in time, from the purchase of the first lot in April 1870 to the end of our study period in December 1959. Lots will appear and disappear as the slider is adjusted.

Visitors can explore the map by clicking on properties and a popup will appear with the history of the property, starting with the earliest transaction. Hovering over properties with the cursor show some details in an information box on the right of your screen and visitors can also use the search box to look for individuals, lot numbers, or other more specific information.

The lots on this map are color coded to some information at a glance. Properties that are owned at any given point in time appear in green and continuously owned properties will look darker as more layers are stacked. Properties known to have been purchased and owned by people of African descent appear outlined in maroon. Using census records and speaking with community members, we have identified 129 deeds where people of African descent purchased or sold land for 22 different properties. Lastly, as this map explores the ways racism and racialization shaped the growth of the Highlands and the lives of those living there, properties with racist and religiously discriminatory restrictive covenants appear in yellow. These restrictive covenants were found in 31 deed records, all conveyed by the Highlands Property Trust from 1936-1950. The restrictions stated the property and buildings on it

shall be used, occupied or enjoyed only by persons of the white race who are of the Christian religion, excepting only servants actually in the employ of the occupant

This information can be seen in the legend in the bottom right of the screen.

The overall goal of the Oak Bluffs Historic Highlands Property map is to make the history of property ownership in the Highlands visible and accessible the present-day community, descendant community members, and community heritage experts, like the African American Heritage Trail of Martha’s Vineyard, the Oak Bluffs Polar Bears, and the Cottagers. Users of the map can explore how the community grew and changed over time, explore this history of the property their family owns, or view where their friends and family lived in the past.

The map is designed to facilitate conversations about these histories, answer questions about property ownership and land transfers, and, eventually, to record memories shared through these discussions. As secondary goal of the map is to facilitate conversations about cultural heritage, preservation, and initiate conversations about utilizing archaeology as another tool with which the community can preserve, learn about, and share its history.

History

The Highlands area of Oak Bluffs was purchased by the wealthy residents who formed Vineyard Grove Company who hoped oping to build a densely populated, thriving, and for them, highly profitable, summer vacation community centered around Methodist summer revivals. In 1870, The Vineyard Grove Company mapped and divided the area into hundreds of small 70-foot by 30-foot parcels in 1870, namming the development the Vineyard Highlands. However, the Methodist Camp Meeting never moved their revivals to the Highlands and the economic rescessions of 1870 depressed propety sales. In response, the Vineyard Grove Company made a deal with a smaller Baptist organization to hold revivals and the community grew slowly over time, with a mix of summer homes and larger institutions.

Historical records and trends in deed records for the Highlands indicate that the earliest residents were predominantly people of European descent from Massachusetts, Rhode Island, Connecticut, and New York, including Baptist preachers and their families. Yet, our research shows that people of African descent were among earliest residents of the Highlands. Identified residents include Dr. Samuel T. Brimingham, who bought property on Beecher Park in August 1870, and Ralph Mitchell, Patience Simmonds, Lucy Matthews, and Mary Ann Mitchell who all bought land in the Highlands between 1887 and 1890. The Baptist Revivals held there introduced new residents to Oak Bluffs and the Highlands community, including Charles and Henrietta Shearer, and by the first decade of the 20th century it had gained a reputation as being a place where Black vacationers could own property, rent homes, and stay in hotels or inns, a reputation that still stands today1.
